## Content Examples — What "Good" Looks Like

### the/SHIFT Blog (Zaradigm) — Organizational Change Communication Article

**URL:** zaradigm.com/organizational-change-communication-leaders-roadmap/
**What it produced:** 5 deliverables from 1 source article — longform blog (2,500+ words), LinkedIn Newsletter, LinkedIn Announcer Post, 2 Substack letters (challenge + framework), 6 social snippets
**Why it's a reference:** One source, multiple format derivatives; editorial learnings captured in [[zaradigm-content-strategy]]

**Key editorial learnings from this piece:**
- Remove horizontal lines (---) — AI writing tell
- Remove em-dashes — per brand kit
- Write key sentences to be quotable in isolation; replace pronouns with nouns
- Use "Hint:" as a label for practical asides — collegial, not preachy
- Frame communication as ONE controllable factor in change success, not the whole game

## Coaching/Facilitation Examples — What Good Looks Like

### the/SHIFT Case Studies (Anonymized, from Operations Doc)

Five reference scenarios used in the/SHIFT labs to demonstrate the quality of facilitation:

**Case 01: "The Swept Rug"** — Managing Up, Crisis Communication, Credibility Under Pressure
Alex (Director) raised a sensitive topic with senior leadership before an all-hands. Leadership skipped it in the meeting. His team is looking to him for answers. He has a 1:1 with the CEO in two days. The question: what does he say to his team TODAY and what does he say to the CEO on Thursday? Reference quality: two competing obligations, time pressure, no clean answer — exactly the kind of scenario that surfaces real leadership communication skill.

**Case 02: "The Silent Promotion"** — Self-Advocacy, Executive Presence, Managing Up
Priya (Senior Manager, 4 years, consistently exceeds expectations) discovers a VP role she didn't know existed went to an external hire. Skip-level tells her "your time will come." She has a performance review in two weeks. Reference quality: forward vs. backward-looking framing, self-advocacy without alienating the people she needs.

**Case 03: "The Meeting After the Meeting"** — Navigating Politics, Influence Without Authority
Marcus (project lead) had his launch plan approved unanimously. Two days later, two senior stakeholders are meeting privately to "revisit some decisions." He can't crash their meeting. What's his move in 48 hours? Reference quality: political savvy vs. manipulation, indirect influence, decision that doesn't control the room.

**Case 04: "The New Leader's Inheritance"** — Tough Conversations, Team Leadership
Jordan (new VP) leads a team that includes Dana, who was also a candidate for the VP role. Dana hasn't said anything directly, but the shift is visible. Jordan needs Dana — she has 12 years of institutional knowledge. Reference quality: navigating interpersonal tension without assuming intent, opening a conversation without triggering defensiveness.

**Case 05: "The Message You Don't Believe"** — Authenticity, Leading Through Change
Sam (Sr. Director) personally disagrees with a restructuring decision she's been asked to communicate. The HR talking points feel sanitized. Her team will see through them. Reference quality: alignment without agreement, how to lead with integrity when you didn't make the call.

## What "Not Good" Looks Like (Anti-Patterns)

Across all content and communications, these are the tells that something has gone wrong:

- **Em-dashes** — AI writing signal; both brands explicitly prohibit
- **"Delve," "tapestry," "landscape," "underscore," "crucial," "pivotal," "showcase," "testament"** — unedited AI output words
- **Generic hooks:** "In today's fast-paced world…", "In an ever-changing landscape…"
- **Filler phrases:** "leverage synergies," "unlock your full potential," "step into your power"
- **Rhetorical questions stacked together** — replace with direct statements
- **Excessive bolding** — decoration instead of emphasis
- **Outcomes disconnected from real behavior** — "go transform your life" instead of "try this in your next 1:1"
- **Condescending over-explanation** — explaining "why communication matters" to senior professionals
