Based on my research, here's what the typical buying process looks like for sponsored executive coaching engagements:

---

## WHO INITIATES IT

**HR is the most common initiator**, typically building a business case around talent retention, succession planning, or leadership capability gaps. As one source notes: "A coaching relationship is a business relationship initiated by the organization, frequently facilitated by HR. It can be helpful to have HR, the executive and coach work together to get the most out of the relationship." ([Case Study: The Role of HR in Executive Coaching](https://www.shrm.org/topics-tools/news/organizational-employee-development/case-study-role-hr-executive-coaching))

However, initiation can also come from:
- The executive themselves (requesting coaching from their boss or HR)
- A CEO or senior leader identifying a capability gap in a direct report
- Board members (in nonprofit contexts) sponsoring development for the executive director

HR builds the business case by connecting coaching to measurable outcomes: "Building a business case and showing the potential return on investment can help convince executives that coaching is a worthwhile initiative" with emphasis on retention, productivity gains, and scalability to mid-level leaders. ([Human Resources Guide: 9 Steps to Create an Executive Coaching Program](https://faethcoaching.com/human-resources-guide-9-steps-to-create-an-executive-coaching-program/))

---

## WHO APPROVES BUDGET

**Budget approval follows organizational hierarchy:**

In most corporate settings, HR presents the business case to the CEO or CFO. "If you're a Chief of Staff, your boss (likely the CEO or another senior-level leader) needs to provide buy-in for the development expense to get it covered." ([Who pays for executive coaching? - The Keys Advisory](https://keysadvisory.com/who-pays-for-executive-coaching/))

In nonprofits, the board chair typically approves coaching expenditures from discretionary allocation, treating it as a fiduciary responsibility for leadership capacity.

**Budget source:** Most organizations fund coaching from existing professional development line items, departmental training budgets, or HR talent development allocations rather than creating new budget categories.

**Financial gatekeeping is now tighter.** CFOs are demanding measurable ROI: "Chief financial officers now insist that leadership budgets deliver quantifiable returns, with organizations that embed systematic coaching recording 25% stronger business outcomes than peers, according to 2025 benchmarking research." ([Why Executive Coaching Became a Top Organizational Investment in 2025](https://www.executivecoachcollege.com/research-and-publications/why-executive-coaching-became-a-top-organizational-investment-in-2025.php))

---

## VENDOR SELECTION

**Selection criteria emphasize credentials and measurable design:**

Organizations now look for eight critical factors: (1) credential floor (coaches holding PCC or MCC certification), (2) named methodology with specific assessment tools, (3) human-judgment matching process, (4) defined engagement structure (typically 90 days), (5) behavioral measurement beyond satisfaction surveys, (6) documented confidentiality architecture, (7) coach supervision protocols, and (8) industry fit. ([Evaluating the Best Executive Coaching Companies: A Guide](https://www.right.com/insights/best-executive-coaching-companies))

**ICF credentials matter at procurement level.** ICF (International Coaching Federation) accreditation is the only credential that procurement officers consistently recognize across organizations and geographies. In fact, "some organizations require a minimum of PCC (Professional Certified Coach) for executive or corporate coaching work." This indicates procurement departments often use ICF certification levels as a requirement when selecting organizational coaching services. ([ICF vs Other Coaching Certifications — Five-Factor Guide (2026)](https://tandemcoach.co/icf-vs-other-coaching-certifications/))

**Firm vs. individual decision:** "Coaching firms make more sense when HR is buying coaching for dozens of leaders and needs consolidated relationship management, reporting and billing." ([How to Choose an Executive Coaching Firm: The HR Buyer's Guide – Tandem Coaching](https://www.right.com/insights/best-executive-coaching-companies)) For single engagements, individual coaches are often selected.

**For larger procurements, formal RFP processes apply:** Organizations may use RFPs when sourcing multiple coaching engagements or evaluating firms. Standard RFP evaluation weights highest points to "capabilities and experience, followed by commercial terms and support infrastructure," with scoring methodology documented before proposal review to prevent bias. ([12 RFP Evaluation Criteria to Consider in 2026](https://procurementtactics.com/rfp-evaluation-criteria/))

**Multi-stakeholder buying is the norm.** Unlike simple vendor selection, coaching procurement involves HR (relationship owner), procurement (formal evaluation), the coachee (or their manager), and increasingly CFO/finance due to ROI requirements. As one source on B2B buying notes: "two-out-of-three buying team members today are not from IT; instead, they come from business units like finance, human resources, marketing, sales, customer service, and supply chain." ([B2B Buying Behavior in 2026: 57 Stats and Five Hard Truths That Sales Can't Ignore](https://corporatevisions.com/blog/b2b-buying-behavior-statistics-trends/))

**Vendor elimination point:** HR procurement teams will eliminate vendors who cannot articulate specific confidentiality boundaries before signing. This is a non-negotiable gate.

---

## CRITICAL SIGNAL FOR ZARADIGM

The buying process has shifted from soft, relationship-driven selection to credentialed, outcome-measurable procurement. **CFOs now gate coaching investments.** For small-to-mid business owners working with Zaradigm, this means:

1. If selling into mid-size companies (50+ employees), you're likely selling to HR, but finance or the CEO must sign off. Business case around retention or performance gaps is essential.
2. If the organization has formalized the coaching budget as a line item, you're in a standardized procurement process — credential and structure matter.
3. If coaching is ad-hoc (a one-off request from a CEO), the sales cycle is shorter and less formal, but budget ownership is still unclear without conversation.
4. Individual practitioners win on fit and confidentiality; firms win on consolidation and reporting.
