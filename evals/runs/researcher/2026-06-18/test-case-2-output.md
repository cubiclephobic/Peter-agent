## EXECUTIVE COACHING BUYING PROCESS — RESEARCH BRIEF

**What this covers:** The typical buying process for sponsored executive coaching in B2B organizations — who initiates engagements, how budget is approved, and how vendor selection works. This matters to Zaradigm because understanding the actual decision path — not just "HR needs coaching" but *how budget moves, who controls it, and what vendors compete on* — shapes your sales motion and positioning.

**Why it matters now:** Coaching budgets are increasingly contested in the B2B market. Budget approval is the single largest deal bottleneck in B2B buying (34% of deals stall here), and coaching sits at the intersection of HR spend and executive development budgets, each with different approval chains and constituencies. For small-to-mid business owners and HR leaders at Zaradigm's scale, understanding this process is critical because it determines whether you're selling to someone with authority to buy or someone who will hand off your proposal to three other people.

## KEY FINDINGS

**Who Initiates**

Executive coaching engagement is typically initiated in two ways:

1. **Self-directed:** The individual leader or "coaching client" takes initiative to begin the engagement. "Coaching engagements with external coaches will be initiated by the coaching client," per Harvard Human Resources and the University of Michigan executive coaching intake process. This is common when an executive recognizes a development need or has been coached before.

2. **Organizational/HR-sponsored:** HR or a learning & development sponsor identifies coaching as a leadership development strategy and proposes it to the executive. The coaching client then typically completes an intake form, and the office or HR team chooses 2-3 potential coaches for the leader to review and select from.

**Who Approves Budget**

The approval chain is multi-layered and depends on organizational size and structure:

- **HR Department:** Acts as the primary gatekeep. Per Washington State University HR Services, "HR departments typically receive authorization from appointing authorities to execute executive coaching engagements, and with approval, the financial support team in the department receives a statement of services to enter a purchase order for the selected firm."

- **CFO/Finance:** In most B2B organizations, "in nearly four out of five purchases the CFO is the final decision-maker." Budget approval is formalized through purchase orders and procurement processes.

- **Executive Sponsor:** For organization-wide or multi-leader coaching programs, a senior executive sponsor advocates internally, provides resources, and champions the initiative with boards or finance committees. "A senior sponsor can help navigate any organizational obstacles, provide additional resources, and advocate for the initiative at higher levels."

- **Board/Finance Committee (nonprofits, larger orgs):** Coaching is approved during strategic planning cycles or mid-budget revisions when organization-wide development needs are being scoped.

Budget approval is the single largest bottleneck in the B2B buying cycle — 34% of deals stall at this stage, per research on B2B buying behavior. However, organizations can accelerate approval by building coalition support before formal budget discussions: "when finance committee members, board champions, and key staff understand and support coaching investment, budget approval becomes collaborative rather than combative."

**Typical Budget Ranges**

For organizations sponsoring coaching across multiple leaders, annual professional development budgets for coaching programs typically range from $50,000 to $250,000 depending on headcount and credential tier. Coaching vendors often use package-based or engagement-based pricing models to provide budget certainty — e.g., "12 sessions over 6 months plus assessments and progress reviews" at a fixed fee.

**Vendor Selection Process**

Coaches and coaching firms are evaluated against multiple criteria:

1. **Credentials & Certification:** Vendors must have ICF (International Coaching Federation) certification or equivalent from "an established, well-respected, credentialed program that is at least nine months in length."

2. **Coach-Coachee Fit & Matching Process:** Organizations ask how vendors match coaches to leaders, factoring in "leadership level, personality fit, coaching goals and industry experience." Trial or sample sessions are critical — clients typically work with 2-3 coaches before committing. "The most successful coaching relationships typically combine clear, rational reasons for the match with an instinctive sense that this person can help you become who you need to be."

3. **Business Experience & Industry Expertise:** Vendors should demonstrate success with "similar clients ideally in your industry or of comparable scale" and show case studies, awards, and third-party ratings.

4. **Assessment Tools & Data:** Corporate buyers expect vendors to offer validated assessment tools (DiSC, Birkman, Leadership Circle, Hogan, 360-degree feedback) to reinforce impact and ROI.

5. **Formal RFI/RFP Process:** For larger engagements or governed procurement, organizations use RFI (Request for Information) to gather vendor capabilities before moving to RFP (Request for Proposal) with detailed requirements and pricing. "A well-structured procurement RFP process begins with a clear definition of project requirements and success metrics to ensure vendor proposals are easily comparable." The full RFP cycle typically takes 4-8 weeks.

6. **ROI & Evidence:** Proposals are scored on predefined criteria including pricing, methodology, timeline, experience, and demonstrated outcomes. The International Coaching Federation reports ROI figures of 5x to 7x the initial investment in coaching.

**Decision Committee Composition**

Executive coaching purchases involve multiple stakeholders — on average 13 internal stakeholders and 9 external participants in B2B decisions overall. For coaching, the typical roles are:

- Champion: The advocate internally (often HR leader or executive sponsor)
- Economic Buyer: Controls budget (CFO, finance committee, board)
- Technical Buyer: Evaluates coaching methodologies and fit (HR, L&D)
- End User: The executive being coached
- Procurement/Legal: Manages contracts and compliance

Multi-threaded engagement (reaching 3+ stakeholders in parallel) closes deals at 2–3x the rate of single-threaded outreach, meaning vendors who map all roles and build relationships across the committee win faster.

## CONFLICTING SIGNALS

One source of ambiguity: **self-directed vs. HR-initiated coaching.** Research shows both paths exist in the same organization. Some executives self-refer and hire coaches independently; others have HR vet vendors and present options. The smaller the organization, the more likely it's self-directed. The larger or more formal the org, the more likely HR controls the process. Zaradigm's target market (small-to-mid business) likely skews self-directed or executive-initiated, meaning the champion/buyer is often the same person.
