# assumption-audit

Surface and stress-test the assumptions a strategy or plan is quietly resting on, before money or time gets committed. Third step in the Diagnose stage — typically run once a direction is forming, even informally, off the back of `situation-assessment` or `growth-barriers`.

Run as: `/assumption-audit [strategy, plan, or decision being considered]`

---

## Behavior

### 1. Pull out every load-bearing assumption

Read the plan or decision as stated and extract the assumptions it depends on to work — market assumptions, customer behavior assumptions, capacity assumptions, timing assumptions. Look for things treated as fact that are actually beliefs. If the plan came from a `growth-barriers` or `situation-assessment` output in this session, use that directly instead of asking Corinna to restate it.

### 2. Classify each assumption

For each one, place it on two axes:
- **Certainty:** how confident are we this is true, based on real evidence versus gut read?
- **Criticality:** if this assumption is wrong, how much of the plan breaks?

The ones that matter are **low certainty + high criticality** — that's where the plan is exposed.

### 3. Stress-test the exposed ones

For each high-exposure assumption: name the evidence that would confirm it, the evidence that would kill it, and whether that evidence is checkable now (cheaply, before committing) or only after acting.

### 4. Output

**Format (scannable in under five minutes):**

- **Assumptions audited:** short list, one line each.
- **Exposed (low certainty, high criticality):** for each — the assumption, what confirms it, what kills it, and whether it's checkable before committing.
- **Safe to proceed on:** the assumptions that are either well-evidenced or low-stakes if wrong — named explicitly so they're not silently re-litigated later.
- **Recommendation:** what to verify before moving, if anything — not a strategy recommendation, just "check this first" or "clear to proceed."

### 5. Guardrails

- This skill doesn't kill ideas — it de-risks them. End on what to check, not a verdict on whether the plan is good.
- Don't manufacture doubt where evidence is already solid. If an assumption is well-supported, say so and move on — don't pad the audit to look rigorous.
- Never use this skill to second-guess a decision Corinna has already made and acted on. It's a pre-commitment tool, not a retrospective one.
